1. u/Sir_Toccoa [score=1, created=2025-11-12T00:26:39]: If she can, it might help to ask her primary care clinician for a referral to a registered dietitian. A dietitian can tailor a plan that fits her goals and medications. Foods like oats, rice, and bread are higher in carbohydrates, so they can raise blood sugar. Even if the new medicine is helping overall, some meals can still cause bigger after meal rises, and can cause insulin to spike, which can often be a cause for many symptoms associated with diabetes. While she cannot check her insulin levels at home, she can check blood glucose before and about two hours after meals to spot patterns. That simple check can be very helpful. Trying a lower carbohydrate approach for a few weeks could be a gentle experiment. Build meals around nonstarchy vegetables, lean proteins, and healthy fats. Keep portions of grains and starchy foods smaller, if any. Stomach upset is common when starting metformin. Taking it with food, increasing the dose gradually, or asking about the extended release version often helps. If symptoms are strong or do not improve, she should reach out to her prescriber. She should certainly not stop the medicine without guidance though. For cold hands and feet, improving circulation can help. Short walks, light stretching, massage, warm baths, staying hydrated, and warm socks or gloves are all good options.
